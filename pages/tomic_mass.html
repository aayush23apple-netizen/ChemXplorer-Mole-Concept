Atomic Mass

Definition

Atomic mass is the mass of a single atom of an element, expressed in atomic mass units (u) or daltons (Da). It represents the average mass of the isotopes of that element weighted by their natural abundance.

Atomic Mass Unit (u): 1 u = 1.660539 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
This is 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

Isotopes and Atomic Mass

Most elements exist as mixtures of isotopes - atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons. The atomic mass listed in the periodic table is a weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes.

Calculation of Average Atomic Mass

Atomic Mass = Σ (isotope mass × relative abundance)

Examples

  • Carbon: 98.93% ¹²C (12.000 u) + 1.07% ¹³C (13.003 u) = 12.011 u
  • Chlorine: 75.78% ³⁵Cl (34.969 u) + 24.22% ³⁷Cl (36.966 u) = 35.453 u
  • Hydrogen: 99.985% ¹H (1.008 u) + 0.015% ²H (2.014 u) = 1.008 u

Relationship to Atomic Number

Atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in the nucleus. Mass number (A) is the total number of protons and neutrons. The relationship is:

Mass Number (A) = Atomic Number (Z) + Number of Neutrons (N)

Units and Standards

  • Atomic Mass Unit (u or Da): The standard unit for expressing atomic masses
  • Carbon-12 Standard: The mass of one atom of carbon-12 is exactly 12 u
  • Conversion: 1 u = 931.494 MeV/c² (mass-energy equivalence)

Measurement Techniques

Atomic masses are determined using:

  • Mass spectrometry
  • Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)
  • X-ray crystallography
  • Modern spectroscopic methods

Importance in Chemistry

  • Foundation for calculating molar masses
  • Essential for stoichiometric calculations
  • Used in determining molecular formulas
  • Important for understanding isotopic effects

Atomic Mass vs Atomic Weight

The terms "atomic mass" and "atomic weight" are often used interchangeably. However, technically:

  • Atomic mass refers to the mass of a single atom
  • Atomic weight refers to the average atomic mass of all isotopes

Periodic Table Context

The atomic masses in the periodic table are average values based on natural isotopic abundances. These values can vary slightly depending on the source of the element due to different isotopic compositions.
