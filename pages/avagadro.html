Avogadro's Number

Definition

Avogadro's number (N_A) is the number of elementary entities (atoms, molecules, ions, or other particles) in one mole of a substance. It is a fundamental constant in chemistry.

Avogadro's Number: N_A = 6.02214076 × 10²³ mol⁻¹

Origin and History

Named after Italian scientist Amedeo Avogadro (1776-1856), who proposed in 1811 that equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain the same number of molecules. The constant was first determined experimentally by Josef Loschmidt in 1865.

Numerical Value

The currently accepted value is 6.02214076 × 10²³, as defined by the International Committee for Weights and Measures in 2019. For most calculations, we use 6.022 × 10²³ or 6.02 × 10²³.

Significance in Chemistry

  • Links the macroscopic world (grams, liters) to the microscopic world (atoms, molecules)
  • Essential for stoichiometric calculations
  • Used in determining molecular weights and formulas
  • Foundation for the mole concept

Applications

Calculating number of particles: Number of particles = moles × N_A

Calculating moles from particles: Moles = Number of particles ÷ N_A

Examples

  • 1 mole of carbon atoms = 6.022 × 10²³ carbon atoms
  • 0.5 moles of oxygen molecules = 3.011 × 10²³ O₂ molecules
  • 2 moles of sodium ions = 1.204 × 10²⁴ Na⁺ ions

Determination Methods

Avogadro's number can be determined through:

  • Electrolytic methods (Faraday's laws)
  • X-ray crystallography
  • Millikan's oil drop experiment (for electron charge)
  • Modern spectroscopic methods

Avogadro's Hypothesis

Equal volumes of all gases, at the same temperature and pressure, contain the same number of molecules. This hypothesis is the basis for understanding gas behavior and led to the development of the kinetic theory of gases.
