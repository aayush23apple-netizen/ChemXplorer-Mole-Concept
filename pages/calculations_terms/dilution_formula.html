The Dilution Formula

When a concentrated solution is diluted by adding more solvent, the concentration changes but the total amount of solute remains the same. This relationship is expressed by the dilution formula:

M₁V₁ = M₂V₂

Where:

  • M₁ = Initial concentration (M)
  • V₁ = Initial volume (L)
  • M₂ = Final concentration (M)
  • V₂ = Final volume (L)

How to Use

Enter known values and leave the unknown variable blank. The calculator will solve for the missing variable.

Note: All volumes must be in the same units (usually liters) and concentrations in molarity (M).

Examples

  • Example 1: How much water to add to 100 mL of 2M HCl to make 0.5M solution?
    M₁ = 2M, V₁ = 0.1L, M₂ = 0.5M, V₂ = ?
    V₂ = (2 × 0.1) ÷ 0.5 = 0.4 L (add 0.3 L water)
  • Example 2: What is the concentration of 500 mL solution made from 50 mL of 3M NaOH?
    M₁ = 3M, V₁ = 0.05L, V₂ = 0.5L, M₂ = ?
    M₂ = (3 × 0.05) ÷ 0.5 = 0.3 M
  • Example 3: What volume of 6M HCl is needed to make 2L of 1.5M solution?
    M₁ = 6M, M₂ = 1.5M, V₂ = 2L, V₁ = ?
    V₁ = (1.5 × 2) ÷ 6 = 0.5 L

Important Notes

  • The dilution formula assumes the volumes are additive
  • Temperature should be constant during dilution
  • This formula applies to molar concentrations
  • For very concentrated solutions, volume changes may occur

Practical Applications

  • Preparing solutions of desired concentrations in labs
  • Serial dilution techniques in microbiology
  • Medicine dosage calculations
  • Industrial chemical processing
