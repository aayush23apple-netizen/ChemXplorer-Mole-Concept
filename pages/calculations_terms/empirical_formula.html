What is Empirical Formula?

The empirical formula is the simplest whole number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound. It shows the relative number of atoms of each element in the compound.

Key Points:
• Simplest ratio of atoms
• May or may not be the same as molecular formula
• Based on experimental data (mass percentages or masses)

Calculation Steps

  1. Convert mass percentages to grams (assume 100g sample)
  2. Convert grams to moles using molar masses
  3. Divide by smallest number of moles
  4. Multiply by smallest integer to get whole numbers

Formula

Empirical Formula = Simplest whole number ratio of atoms

Examples

Example 1: From Mass Percentages

A compound contains 40.0% C, 6.7% H, and 53.3% O.

  1. Assume 100g: 40.0g C, 6.7g H, 53.3g O
  2. Moles: C = 40.0/12 = 3.33, H = 6.7/1 = 6.7, O = 53.3/16 = 3.33
  3. Divide by 3.33: C = 1, H = 2, O = 1
  4. Empirical formula: CH₂O

Example 2: From Experimental Masses

Analysis shows 2.40g C and 0.40g H in a compound.

  1. Moles: C = 2.40/12 = 0.20, H = 0.40/1 = 0.40
  2. Divide by 0.20: C = 1, H = 2
  3. Empirical formula: CH₂

Important Notes

  • Use atomic masses from the periodic table
  • Round to appropriate significant figures
  • If ratios are not whole numbers, multiply by a small integer
  • Empirical formula may differ from molecular formula
