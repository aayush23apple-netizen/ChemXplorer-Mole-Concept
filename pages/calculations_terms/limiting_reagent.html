What is a Limiting Reagent?

The limiting reagent (or limiting reactant) is the reactant that is completely consumed in a chemical reaction. It determines the maximum amount of product that can be formed. The other reactants are present in excess.

Key Concept: The reaction stops when the limiting reagent is used up, regardless of how much of the other reactants remain.

How to Identify Limiting Reagent

  1. Write the balanced chemical equation
  2. Calculate moles of each reactant
  3. Determine mole ratios from the equation
  4. Compare actual mole ratios with required ratios
  5. The reactant that gives the smallest product amount is limiting

Formula

Moles required = (Moles available × Coefficient of required reactant) ÷ Coefficient of available reactant

Examples

Example 1: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl

If we have 3 moles of H₂ and 2 moles of Cl₂:

  • For H₂: moles of Cl₂ required = (3 × 1) ÷ 1 = 3 moles
  • For Cl₂: moles of H₂ required = (2 × 1) ÷ 1 = 2 moles
  • Since we have only 2 moles of H₂ but need 3 moles of Cl₂, H₂ is limiting

Example 2: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

If we have 4 moles of H₂ and 3 moles of O₂:

  • For H₂: moles of O₂ required = (4 × 1) ÷ 2 = 2 moles
  • For O₂: moles of H₂ required = (3 × 2) ÷ 1 = 6 moles
  • Since we have only 4 moles of H₂ but need 6 moles, O₂ is limiting

Importance in Chemistry

  • Determines maximum yield of products
  • Important for industrial processes
  • Essential for stoichiometric calculations
  • Helps optimize reaction conditions

Practical Applications

  • Chemical manufacturing
  • Pharmaceutical production
  • Food processing
  • Environmental chemistry

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting to use mole ratios from the balanced equation
  • Not converting masses to moles first
  • Confusing limiting reagent with excess reagent
  • Not considering all reactants in the equation
